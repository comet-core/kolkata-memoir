The alarm was not mine to set. My mama knocked at 4:30 AM with a plan — Ganga Ghat, sunrise, the whole thing. It took me a solid twenty minutes to fully reassemble myself into a functioning person, but I was genuinely on board. The main goal was to catch the sunrise, but Kolkata had other ideas. The sky outside was a flat, unbroken grey. The clouds had arrived before us and had no intention of moving. Just like that, the sunrise plan quietly dissolved before it had even properly begun.

So we pivoted to the original plan anyway — walk to the Ganga Ghat. The compound was still completely asleep. Three dogs had taken full ownership of the open ground, the Peerless Nagar gate looked entirely different at this hour, and the whole place had a stillness to it that you genuinely cannot get at any other time of day. There is something about a familiar space before the world has filled it back up.

Outside the compound, the road was near-empty. And then I saw something that genuinely stopped me — people standing patiently at a traffic signal, waiting for the green, on a road that had maybe two vehicles on it. No pressure, no reason to wait, just the habit of doing things right. Coming from Mumbai, that was one of the stranger and more quietly impressive sights of the entire trip.

Somewhere en route to the Ghat, my mama had a change of heart. Sealdah, he said. We have time, it will be fun. I was not immediately sold on the idea of a U-turn, but that is the thing about early mornings with someone who knows a city well — eventually you just stop questioning and follow. We turned around and walked straight toward Sodepur station instead, through roads that were still, for the most part, entirely ours.

We got off at Sealdah with no plan in particular, which turned out to be exactly the right approach. My mama bought tea toast from a stall right outside the station -- rusk bread you dip into hot chai before eating. It sounds like nothing, but at 6 AM after two hours of walking, it was the best possible thing. The hot tea finished the job of waking me up that the grey sky had failed to do.

From there we walked out toward the main road, talking the whole way. Somewhere between the station and the street, my mama started walking me through Monte Carlo methods — probability, randomness, how you simulate outcomes by just throwing enough numbers at a problem. It was the kind of explanation that feels almost surreal at 6 in the morning, but somehow makes perfect sense when your brain has no defenses up and there is nothing else competing for your attention.

The main road had the same quiet energy as Sodepur but on a bigger canvas — wider streets, more buildings, still only a handful of people moving through it. We had the bold idea to walk all the way to College Street, about fifteen to twenty minutes away. Neither of us had cash, and Kolkata's relationship with UPI is still, to put it charitably, a work in progress, so a bus was not an option. We walked.

It was my first time in this part of the city, and there was something quietly exciting about that — unfamiliar streets, new geometry, a Kolkata I had not seen before. Nearing College Street, hunger started making itself known, and we stopped at a roadside stall that was already open and clearly knew what it was doing.

After that we wandered around College Street for a while — taking in the empty pavements, watching the occasional car cut through the wide roads, looking at the bookshops that would look completely different in an hour. Eventually we decided to head back, caught a Rapido, and were back at Sodepur by around 8. The walk from the station back to Peerless Nagar was familiar enough by now to feel like coming home.

---

The afternoon shifted gears entirely. My mom's elder sister had arrived to stay for a bit, and suddenly the apartment was full again. We spent the time playing raja, mantri, chor, sipahi — six people, custom rules, and a completely unreasonable amount of competitive energy. It was the kind of afternoon that sneaks up on you and turns out to be one of the best parts of the trip.

By evening, the sky had turned. The water tower at the end of the lane was silhouetted against a wash of orange and purple, and the whole neighbourhood looked like a different place entirely.

My mama had gone out for a walk earlier and asked me to join when I could. I was in the middle of some laptop work and said I'd come down once I was done. By the time I headed out, he had walked over to Sriniketan — a five-minute stretch from the gate — and was there with his wife. We went into a sweet shop nearby. I had gulab jamun. That was enough.

Later, just the two of us peeled off to a restaurant called Punjabi Food Junction for dinner. Paneer tikka to start, then two naans and lemon chicken. My mama had a lassi and watched me eat. The painting on the wall behind us was loud and warm, all deep reds and oranges, which felt exactly right for the end of a day that had started at 4:30 in the morning.
